# Methods

## 1. Overview of the Computational Framework

A multi-physics computational framework was developed in Python to simulate the magnetic capture of SPION-labelled endothelial cells by a magnetised stent, and to predict the subsequent paracrine VEGF signalling field generated by captured cells. The framework is structured in three hierarchical modules: (i) the magnetic field and force model, which computes the spatially varying field produced by the stent and the resulting force on circulating cells; (ii) the capture and trajectory module, which evaluates whether individual cells are retained at the stent surface under competing hydrodynamic drag; and (iii) the paracrine transport module, which predicts the steady-state and transient VEGF concentration field generated by the captured cell population. All physical parameters and their literature sources are summarised in Tables 1–3.

---

## 2. Stent Geometry

The stent was modelled as a ring of *n* uniformly magnetised rectangular prisms (struts) arranged symmetrically at radius *R* in the z = 0 plane (the stent midplane). Each strut has three half-dimensions:

- **a = t/2** — radial half-thickness (local x-axis, pointing radially outward)
- **b = w/2** — circumferential half-width (local y-axis)
- **c = L/2** — axial half-length (local z-axis)

Strut centres are located at **(R cos θᵢ, R sin θᵢ, 0)** for θᵢ = 2πi/n, i = 0, 1, …, n−1. The nominal stent geometry (V2-2C configuration) is given in Table 1.

**Table 1. Stent geometry parameters.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Value | Unit |
|-----------|--------|-------|------|
| Ring radius | R | 1.5 | mm |
| Strut circumferential width | w | 100 | µm |
| Strut radial thickness | t | 80 | µm |
| Strut axial length | L | 500 | µm |
| Number of struts | n | 12 | — |

## 3. Magnetic Field Model

### 3.1 Analytical Stent Field — Akoun & Yonnet Kernel

The magnetic flux density produced by each strut was computed using the exact closed-form analytical expressions derived by Akoun & Yonnet (1984), as formalised by Furlani (2001). The derivation treats each rectangular prism as a distribution of surface magnetic charge density σ_m = **M** · **n̂** on its six faces. The resulting scalar magnetic potential integrates analytically to a sum of arctan and logarithm terms evaluated at the eight corners of the prism.

For a prism centred at the origin with half-dimensions (a, b, c) and magnetisation components (Mₓ, My, Mz), the kernel evaluates:

$$F(U, V, W) = U \ln(V + R) + V \ln(U + R) - W \arctan\!\left(\frac{UV}{WR}\right)$$

where R = (U² + V² + W²)^(1/2), and (U, V, W) are corner-displaced observation coordinates. Summing over all eight corners with alternating signs (−1)^(i+j+k) and taking **H** = −∇φ_m gives the field components in the local prism frame. The exterior field is **B** = μ₀**H**; the interior correction (+ μ₀**M** along the magnetisation axis) was not applied, as all observation points are outside the strut material.

For each strut *i*, the observation point is first transformed from the global frame into the local prism frame by rotation **R_i^T**, the local field is computed via the kernel, and the result is rotated back to the global frame:

$$\mathbf{B}_i^{\text{global}} = \mathbf{R}_i \, \mathbf{B}_i^{\text{local}}$$

The total stent field is the superposition over all *n* struts:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\text{stent}}(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{B}_i^{\text{global}}(\mathbf{r})$$

The magnetisation direction was set to radial (local +x) for all parametric studies, consistent with the radial magnetisation that arises when a ferromagnetic stent is placed in an axially applied external field.

### 3.2 External Field and Superposition

A spatially uniform external field **B₀** = (0, 0, B₀_z) was applied to represent an MRI-strength background field. The total field at any observation point is:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}(\mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{B}_{\text{stent}}(\mathbf{r}) + \mathbf{B}_0$$

For simulations representing MRI conditions, B₀ = 1.5 T was used axially (along z, parallel to the vessel axis). Because the external field is spatially uniform, ∇**B₀** = 0, but the gradient of the total field *magnitude* is non-trivially modified. Even though ∇|**B₀**| = 0, the scalar gradient satisfies:

$$\frac{\partial |\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}|}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}} \cdot \partial \mathbf{B}_{\text{stent}} / \partial x_i}{|\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}|}$$

Consequently, when **B₀** is axial and **B_stent** is primarily radial, the projection suppresses |∇|**B_total**|| relative to |∇|**B_stent**|| alone. The physically correct capture metric is therefore the *force parameter* FP = |**B_total**| · |∇|**B_total**|| (units T²/m), not the gradient alone.

### 3.3 COMSOL Calibration

The analytical permanent-magnet model approximates the behaviour of a soft-ferromagnetic stent (relative permeability μᵣ ≈ 2) computed by finite element analysis in COMSOL Multiphysics. Two calibration points were established by matching the gradient threshold crossings of the analytical model to the COMSOL V2-2C geometry:

- **B₀ = 0:** Effective magnetisation M_eff = 0.619 MA/m reproduces the COMSOL 100 T/m threshold crossing at 0.240 mm from the stent surface.
- **B₀ = 1.5 T:** Effective magnetisation M_eff,1.5T = 2.20 MA/m reproduces both the 100 T/m crossing (0.240 mm) and 40 T/m crossing (0.380 mm) with < 1% error.

The ratio M_eff,1.5T / M_eff ≈ 3.55 quantifies the amplification of the effective magnetisation — and therefore the capture range — due to the applied MRI field.

### 3.4 Field Gradient Computation

All field gradients were computed numerically using central finite differences with step size δ = 500 nm, applied to the `field_at` interface of the relevant field object (stent only, or total field including B₀). The gradient of the field magnitude is:

$$\nabla|\mathbf{B}|(\mathbf{r}) \approx \frac{|\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r} + \delta\hat{e}_k)| - |\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r} - \delta\hat{e}_k)|}{2\delta} \hat{e}_k, \quad k = x, y, z$$

The vector gradient of **B** (used in the force calculation) was similarly computed component-by-component and used to evaluate the force direction.

## 4. SPION-Labelled Cell Model

Circulating endothelial cells labelled with superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs) were modelled as spherical particles of radius R_cell = 10 µm carrying a payload of SPION material. The SPION volume within the cell is:

$$V_{\text{SPION}} = \frac{m_{\text{SPION}}}{\rho_{\text{SPION}}}$$

where m_SPION is the iron oxide mass per cell (kg) and ρ_SPION = 5170 kg/m³ is the density of magnetite (Fe₃O₄). The default SPION loading was 50 pg per cell, representative of the Polyak et al. (2008) stent-targeting protocol. Parametric sweeps over SPION loading were performed over the range 1–300 pg/cell (log-spaced), spanning literature values from Polyak et al. (2008) (10–200 pg) and Chorny et al. (2007) (50 pg typical).

**Table 2. SPION-labelled cell parameters.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Value | Unit | Reference |
|-----------|--------|-------|------|-----------|
| Cell radius | R_cell | 10 | µm | — |
| SPION mass per cell (default) | m_SPION | 50 | pg | Polyak et al. 2008 |
| SPION volume susceptibility | χ_SPION | 2.0 | — | Furlani & Ng 2006 |
| SPION material density (magnetite) | ρ_SPION | 5170 | kg/m³ | — |
| SPION saturation magnetisation (bulk magnetite) | M_sat | 446 × 10³ | A/m | Furlani & Ng 2006 |

### 4.1 Langevin Saturation Model

The SPION susceptibility was treated as field-dependent to account for saturation near the stent surface, where local field magnitudes can exceed 100 mT. An effective susceptibility χ_eff(B) was computed using the Langevin superparamagnetic model:

$$\chi_{\text{eff}}(B) = \chi_0 \cdot f_{\text{sat}}(B)$$

where the saturation factor is:

$$f_{\text{sat}}(\xi) = \frac{3 \mathcal{L}(\xi)}{\xi}, \quad \mathcal{L}(\xi) = \coth(\xi) - \frac{1}{\xi}$$

with the dimensionless field parameter ξ = αB/μ₀ and α = 3χ₀/M_sat. At low fields (ξ → 0), f_sat → 1 and χ_eff → χ₀ (linear regime). At high fields, M saturates and χ_eff decreases as f_sat → 3/ξ → 0. A Taylor expansion χ_eff ≈ χ₀(1 − ξ²/15) was used for numerical stability at small ξ < 10⁻³.

## 5. Magnetic Force on a Cell

The magnetic force on a SPION-labelled cell was computed using the gradient-force expression derived by Furlani & Ng (2006) from the energy of a magnetic dipole in a non-uniform field. For a spherical particle with volume susceptibility χ_SPION in a curl-free field region (∇ × **B** = 0 in free space):

$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} = \frac{V_{\text{SPION}} \, \chi_{\text{eff}}}{\mu_0} \, |\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}| \, \nabla|\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}|$$

The direction of **F_mag** is along ∇|**B_total**|, pointing from regions of lower to higher field magnitude — i.e., toward the stent strut surface. The magnitude is:

$$|\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}}| = \frac{V_{\text{SPION}} \, \chi_{\text{eff}}}{\mu_0} \, |\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}| \cdot |\nabla|\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}||$$

Equivalently, the force is the gradient of the magnetic energy density:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}} = \frac{V_{\text{SPION}} \, \chi_{\text{eff}}}{2 \mu_0} \, \nabla|\mathbf{B}_{\text{total}}|^2$$

The **force parameter** FP(r) = |**B_total**(r)| · |∇|**B_total**(r)|| (T²/m) is the field-geometry quantity that scales linearly with the SPION volume and susceptibility to give the physical force.

## 6. Hydrodynamic Drag

### 6.1 Blood Flow Model

Blood flow was modelled as steady, laminar, Newtonian Poiseuille flow in a cylindrical vessel of radius R_vessel = 1.54 mm (matching the stent outer surface radius R + t/2). The axial velocity profile is:

$$v_z(r) = 2 \bar{v} \left(1 - \frac{r^2}{R_{\text{vessel}}^2}\right), \quad r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$$

where v̄ is the cross-sectional mean velocity. The peak velocity at the vessel centreline is v_max = 2v̄. Three representative mean velocities were used in parametric studies: 0.05, 0.2, and 0.5 m/s, spanning from slow (post-stenotic or diseased-vessel) to fast (healthy MCA) flows.

The Womersley number for the MCA at physiological pulsatility is Wo ≈ 2, justifying a quasi-steady Poiseuille approximation for time-averaged capture analysis. Blood viscosity was taken as η = 4 mPa·s (whole blood, haematocrit ~40%).

**Table 3. Haemodynamic parameters.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Value | Unit | Reference |
|-----------|--------|-------|------|-----------|
| Vessel radius | R_vessel | 1.54 | mm | — |
| Mean velocity (representative) | v̄ | 0.2 | m/s | Aaslid et al. 1982 |
| Blood dynamic viscosity | η | 4 | mPa·s | — |
| Blood density | ρ | 1060 | kg/m³ | — |

### 6.2 Stokes Drag

The viscous drag on a spherical cell moving at velocity **v_cell** through blood at local velocity **v_blood** was computed using the Stokes relation:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{drag}} = 6\pi \eta R_{\text{cell}} \, (\mathbf{v}_{\text{blood}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{cell}})$$

For the static capture criterion (Section 7), cells were assumed stationary (**v_cell** = 0), giving the maximum possible drag force at each location. The particle Reynolds number at physiological conditions, Re = ρ v d / η ≈ 1.1, confirms that inertia is negligible and the Stokes approximation is valid.

## 7. Static Capture Criterion

A cell at position **r** is considered capturable if the magnetic force toward the stent exceeds the hydrodynamic drag resisting it:

$$|\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}}(\mathbf{r})| > |\mathbf{F}_{\text{drag}}(\mathbf{r})| \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{captured}$$

This is the conservative scalar criterion of Furlani & Ng (2006, Eq. 14). It is conservative because in general the forces are not coaxial: **F_mag** is primarily radial (toward the strut) while **F_drag** is primarily axial (along the flow). A vector comparison would allow capture even when |**F_mag**| < |**F_drag**| if the radial component alone is sufficient to draw the cell to the surface. The scalar criterion therefore under-estimates the true capture zone; the trajectory integration model (Section 8) resolves this limitation by treating the forces correctly in 3D.

The **capture distance** is defined as the outermost distance from the stent inner surface (r = R − t/2) along a radial inward sweep at which the capture criterion is satisfied. This was computed by evaluating the criterion at 400 equally spaced points along the through-strut radial line from r = 2 µm to r = R − t/2, and returning the maximum distance at which capture was last predicted.

The **capture margin** — the signed difference |**F_mag**| − |**F_drag**| — was computed at all observation points and used to map the spatial extent of the capture zone as a function of SPION loading and blood velocity.

## 8. Trajectory Integration

### 8.1 Equation of Motion

For a SPION-labelled cell in the low Reynolds number regime (Re ≪ 1), inertia is negligible and the cell velocity at every instant equals the terminal velocity where magnetic force and Stokes drag balance. The cell trajectory satisfies the first-order ODE:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{blood}}(\mathbf{r}) + \frac{\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}}(\mathbf{r})}{6\pi \eta R_{\text{cell}}}$$

The second term is the magnetic drift velocity — the radial velocity component induced by the field gradient that drives the cell toward the stent.

### 8.2 Numerical Integration

Trajectories were integrated using the Runge-Kutta RK45 method with adaptive step-size control, as implemented in `scipy.integrate.solve_ivp`. Default tolerances were rtol = 10⁻⁶ and atol = 10⁻⁹. Integration proceeded from the injection position upstream (z < 0) until one of three terminal events fired:

1. **Escape**: the cell reached the downstream plane z = z_end = 2 mm without contacting the stent.
2. **Strut proximity**: the 3D distance from the cell centre to any strut centre fell below the strut surface radius (max(w, t)/2 = 50 µm) plus a capture tolerance of 5 µm.
3. **Wall contact**: the radial position r_xy = (x² + y²)^(1/2) reached the stent inner-surface radius R − t/2 ≈ 1.46 mm.

Outcomes were classified as **captured** (events 2 or 3), **escaped** (event 1), or **error** (solver failure or timeout).

### 8.3 Injection Protocol and Capture Efficiency

For population-level capture efficiency, cells were injected at N evenly-spaced positions along a diametric line segment across the vessel lumen. Capture efficiency was defined as:

$$\eta_{\text{cap}} = \frac{N_{\text{captured}}}{N_{\text{total}}}$$

Parametric sweeps over mean blood velocity and SPION loading were performed using independent trajectories per parameter point. Parallel computation was implemented via `multiprocessing.Pool` using all available CPU cores.

## 9. Paracrine VEGF Transport Model

### 9.1 Coordinate System and Domain

The paracrine model operates in a 2D plane representing the unrolled inner surface of the vessel wall. Two spatial coordinates are used:

- **x** — circumferential arc coordinate (m), spanning 0 to 2πR (the full stent circumference)
- **z** — axial coordinate (m), spanning the length of the domain

Boundary conditions are periodic in x (the circumference closes on itself) and zero-flux (Neumann) at both axial ends (z = 0 and z = Lz), representing no VEGF flux through the vessel ends of the domain. The domain size was set to Lx = 2πR ≈ 9.42 mm (full circumference) and Lz = 4L = 2 mm (four stent lengths axially) for all VEGF transport simulations.

### 9.2 VEGF Source Field

Each captured cell was modelled as a Gaussian volumetric source of VEGF:

$$S_i(x, z) = q_{\text{vol}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - x_i)^2 + (z - z_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

where σ = 10 µm is the cell radius (Gaussian half-width) and (xᵢ, zᵢ) is the cell position. The peak volumetric secretion rate q_vol is derived from the per-cell secretion rate q_cell (g/s):

$$q_{\text{vol}} = \frac{q_{\text{cell}}}{2\pi \sigma^2 h} \times 10^3 \quad \text{[ng mL}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}\text{]}$$

where h = 20 µm is the effective tissue slab thickness (approximately two cell layers), and the factor 10³ converts from g/m³ to ng/mL. The total source field is the superposition over all N_cells captured cells:

$$S(x, z) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{cells}}} S_i(x, z)$$

Per-cell secretion rates were parameterised using the Ozawa et al. (2004) classification of VEGF production by endothelial cell populations. Ozawa et al. report population-level rates in ng per 10⁶ cells per day; these were converted to per-cell mass rates (g/s) as:

$$q_{\text{cell}} = \frac{Q_{\text{pop}} \times 10^{-9}}{10^6 \times 86400} \quad \text{[g s}^{-1}\text{]}$$

where Q_pop is the population rate in ng/10⁶ cells/day. Ozawa et al. classify cell populations as: *normal angiogenesis* (5–70 ng/10⁶ cells/day), *threshold angiogenesis* (70–100 ng/10⁶ cells/day), and *aberrant angiogenesis* (> 100 ng/10⁶ cells/day). The representative simulation rates used were 37.5 ng/10⁶ cells/day (midpoint of normal) and 85 ng/10⁶ cells/day (midpoint of threshold).

**Table 4. VEGF secretion parameters.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Value | Unit | Reference |
|-----------|--------|-------|------|-----------|
| Per-cell secretion rate (Yen et al.) | q_cell | 5.08 × 10⁻²¹ | g/s | Yen et al. 2011 |
| Gaussian source half-width (cell radius) | σ | 10 | µm | — |
| Effective tissue slab thickness | h | 20 | µm | — |
| Ozawa normal midpoint rate | Q_pop | 37.5 | ng/10⁶ cells/day | Ozawa et al. 2004 |
| Ozawa threshold midpoint rate | Q_pop | 85.0 | ng/10⁶ cells/day | Ozawa et al. 2004 |
| VEGF molecular weight | M_W | 45,000 | g/mol | — |

Cell positions in the 2D domain were derived from the 3D stent geometry by mapping cylindrical strut coordinates to circumferential arc position: xᵢ = R · arctan2(cy, cx) mod 2πR. For multi-cell simulations, additional cells per strut were distributed by adding Gaussian-distributed random offsets (σ_spread = 50 µm) around each strut position, representing the spatial spread of cells captured near each strut.

### 9.3 Reaction–Diffusion–Advection Equation

VEGF transport in the tissue was governed by the 2D reaction–diffusion–advection (RDA) equation:

$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \left(\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial z^2}\right) - u_z \frac{\partial C}{\partial z} + S(x, z) - k \, C$$

where C(x, z, t) is the VEGF concentration (ng/mL), D is the effective tissue diffusion coefficient (m²/s), u_z is the tissue interstitial flow velocity in the axial direction (m/s), S(x, z) is the volumetric source field (ng mL⁻¹ s⁻¹), and k is the first-order VEGF degradation rate (s⁻¹).

The characteristic VEGF diffusion length, L_D = (D/k)^(1/2) ≈ 734 µm, sets the spatial scale over which VEGF from a point source decays to 1/e of its maximum value.

**Table 5. VEGF transport parameters.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Value | Unit | Reference |
|-----------|--------|-------|------|-----------|
| Diffusion coefficient (VEGF₁₆₅ in ECM, in vivo) | D | 1.04 × 10⁻¹⁰ | m²/s | Mac Gabhann & Popel 2006 |
| First-order degradation rate | k | 1.93 × 10⁻⁴ | s⁻¹ | Kleinheinz et al. 2010 |
| Characteristic diffusion length | L_D = (D/k)^(1/2) | 734 | µm | — |
| Tissue interstitial velocity (typical) | u_z | 0–100 | µm/s | Piwnica-Worms et al. 1999 |

The Péclet number Pe = u_z · L_D / D characterises the relative importance of advection versus diffusion. For typical interstitial flow (u_z ~ 1 µm/s in normal tissue), Pe ≈ 7, and advection is secondary. In inflamed or pathological tissue (u_z ~ 50–100 µm/s), Pe ≈ 350 and advection can dominate the spatial distribution of VEGF.

### 9.4 Steady-State Solver

At steady state, ∂C/∂t = 0, so the RDA equation reduces to:

$$D\nabla^2 C - u_z \frac{\partial C}{\partial z} - k \, C = -S(x, z)$$

This was discretised on a uniform Cartesian grid (Nx × Nz cells, centred-cell layout) using second-order central finite differences and solved directly as a sparse linear system. The discretisation was constructed using Kronecker products of 1D operators:

$$\mathbf{A} = D \left(\mathbf{L}_x \otimes \mathbf{I}_z + \mathbf{I}_x \otimes \mathbf{L}_z\right) - u_z \left(\mathbf{I}_x \otimes \mathbf{A}_z\right) - k \, \mathbf{I}$$

where **L_x** is the 1D Laplacian in x (periodic boundary conditions), **L_z** is the 1D Laplacian in z (zero-flux ghost cell: the boundary diagonal entry is set to −1 instead of −2, equivalent to reflecting the concentration), and **A_z** is the 1D first-derivative operator in z (central differences with one-sided first-order stencils at boundaries). The flat index mapping is k = i · Nz + j for grid cell (i, j), consistent with the numpy `meshgrid` convention (`indexing='ij'`). The linear system **A c** = −**s** was solved directly using `scipy.sparse.linalg.spsolve` (SuperLU factorisation). All simulations used Nx = Nz = 300 grid points (600 × 600 for high-resolution concentration profile figures), giving cell widths dx = dz ≈ 31 µm.

### 9.5 Transient Solver

Time-dependent solutions were obtained by explicit Euler integration of the RDA equation. The time step was constrained by the combined CFL stability condition for diffusion and advection:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{0.4}{D\left(\frac{1}{\Delta x^2} + \frac{1}{\Delta z^2}\right) + \frac{|u_z|}{\Delta z}}$$

The safety factor 0.4 (relative to the pure diffusion CFL limit of 0.5) provides margin for the advection term. At each time step:

1. The Laplacian in x is computed using second-order central differences with periodic wrapping at x = 0 and x = Lx.
2. The Laplacian in z is computed with zero-flux ghost cells at z = 0 and z = Lz.
3. The advection term −u_z ∂C/∂z is computed using central differences at interior z-points (boundary advection is set to zero).
4. The concentration is updated: C ← C + Δt (D(∇²_x C + ∇²_z C) + Adv + S − k C).
5. Concentrations are clipped to zero (C ← max(C, 0)) to prevent unphysical negative values.

Snapshots of C(x, z, t) were stored at n_snapshots equally spaced time points for subsequent analysis.

## 10. Therapeutic Zone Analysis

Three scalar metrics were derived from the solved VEGF concentration field:

**Peak concentration** C_max = max{C(x, z)} over the domain.

**Therapeutic zone radius** r_tz: the maximum distance from the nearest captured cell at which the concentration exceeds a threshold C_thresh. This is computed as:

$$r_{\text{tz}} = \max\left\{ \min_{i}\left\|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{c}_i\right\|_2 : C(\mathbf{p}) \geq C_{\text{thresh}} \right\}$$

where the maximum is taken over all grid points **p** where C ≥ C_thresh, and the inner minimum is the distance to the nearest captured cell position **c_i**.

**Azimuthally-averaged concentration profile** C̄(r): grid points were binned by distance to the nearest captured cell, and the mean concentration in each radial bin was computed. This profile, C̄(r) vs r, characterises the typical VEGF concentration experienced by tissue at a given distance from the cell population.

**Time to threshold** t_thresh(probe): for transient solutions, the first time at which C at a specified probe position reached C_thresh.

The threshold used for therapeutic zone analysis was C_thresh = 5 ng/mL as an engineering reference value. It is noted that Ozawa et al. (2004) report cell *production* rates (ng per 10⁶ cells per day), not tissue VEGF *concentrations* (ng/mL); no direct literature source establishes a specific tissue VEGF concentration threshold for angiogenesis induction. The 5 ng/mL contour is used as a reference isoline for comparing cell count and secretion rate scenarios, not as a validated therapeutic threshold.

## 11. Parametric Studies

The following parametric sweeps were performed:

**SPION loading sweep** (Fig. 17): SPION loading was varied from 1 to 300 pg/cell (45 log-spaced values). Capture distance and force ratio |F_mag|/|F_drag| were computed at each loading for three mean blood velocities (0.05, 0.2, 0.5 m/s), with B₀ = 1.5 T (MRI). The magnetic force-per-unit-SPION-mass was precomputed once along the radial profile; force at each loading was then obtained by scaling, reducing computation time to ~3–5 s.

**Ozawa secretion rate vs cell count sweep** (Fig. 29): A 2D grid of N_cells × Q_pop was computed, spanning n_per_strut = {5, 10, 20, 40, 80, 160} cells per strut (40–1280 total cells) and Q_pop = 10–150 ng/10⁶ cells/day (15 linearly spaced values). At each point, the steady-state concentration field was solved (Nx = Nz = 300) and C_max and r_tz were recorded.

**Concentration vs distance profiles** (Fig. 23): High-resolution concentration profiles were computed for n_per_strut = 80 (640 total cells) at Normal (37.5 ng/10⁶ cells/day) and Threshold (85 ng/10⁶ cells/day) Ozawa rates on a 600 × 600 grid.

## 12. Software and Reproducibility

The framework was implemented in Python 3.x using NumPy, SciPy (sparse linear algebra, ODE integration), and Matplotlib. All figures were generated programmatically from a single `regenerate_original_results.py` script. The stent magnetic field kernel was verified against the analytical far-field dipole limit. The steady-state RDA solver was validated for isotropy by checking that a single central Gaussian source produces equal concentration values at equal radial distances in x and z (isotropy ratios within 5% at distances 100–500 µm). The transient solver time-step was validated against the CFL stability bound.
