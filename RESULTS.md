## 1. Magnetic Field and Gradient Characterisation

The foundational stage of this work establishes the magnetic field and gradient profile generated by the magnetisable stent under physiologically representative conditions. All results in this section are computed from the Akoun and Yonnet (1984) closed-form analytical kernel, which expresses the three-dimensional magnetic flux density produced by a uniformly magnetised rectangular prism in terms of arctan and logarithmic functions summed over the prism's eight corners. The ring geometry is constructed by superposing *n* = 12 such struts arranged at equal azimuthal intervals at a radial distance of 1.5 mm. The gradient **∇**|**B**| is evaluated by central finite differences with a step size of δ = 500 nm. Unless otherwise stated, no external field is applied (*B*₀ = 0) in this section.

---

### 1.1 Single-Strut Field Profile

**Figure 1** presents the magnetic flux density magnitude |**B**| and gradient magnitude |**∇**|**B**|| as a function of radial distance from the strut surface for a single isolated strut, computed using the full three-dimensional Akoun and Yonnet kernel alongside the long-strut (infinite-length) analytical limit. Two key observations arise from this figure.

First, the field decays steeply with distance: |**B**| falls approximately one order of magnitude within the first 400 µm from the strut surface, consistent with the dipolar near-field character of a finite magnetic prism. Second, the gradient magnitude peaks not at the strut surface but at a small stand-off distance of approximately 20–40 µm, reflecting the spatial derivative of the field; at the surface itself, symmetry partially cancels opposing gradient contributions. The long-strut limit converges to within 5% of the full 3-D solution beyond approximately 100 µm, confirming that the finite-length approximation is adequate for struts with the aspect ratios considered (*L* = 500 µm, *w* = 100 µm, *t* = 80 µm).

This figure establishes the fundamental length scale over which magnetic forces are significant: the effective field range extends to approximately 300–400 µm from the strut axis before the gradient falls below the drag-dominated threshold.

---

### 1.2 Ring Geometry Heatmaps

**Figure 2** displays two-dimensional heatmaps of the magnetic flux density produced by the full twelve-strut ring, shown in (a) a cross-sectional plane perpendicular to the vessel axis and (b) a circumferential unwrapped projection. The ring geometry introduces a spatially periodic modulation absent from the single-strut profile: the field is strongly enhanced in narrow angular sectors immediately adjacent to each strut and falls to near-background levels midway between struts.

The cross-sectional heatmap (Fig. 2a) reveals the azimuthal structure of the field, with twelve local maxima at the strut positions arranged symmetrically at *r* = 1.5 mm. The field magnitude at the inner vessel wall (*r* ≈ 1.3–1.4 mm) reaches 50–200 mT depending on azimuthal position, while the field at the vessel centreline (*r* = 0) falls below 5 mT, confirming that magnetic force is significant only in the near-wall region. The circumferential projection (Fig. 2b) illustrates how cells passing at different axial offsets from the strut mid-plane encounter weaker field gradients, motivating the three-dimensional trajectory analysis of Section 3.

---

### 1.3 Axial Gradient Profile

**Figure 3** shows the axial dependence of the gradient magnitude |**∇**|**B**|| evaluated at a fixed radial position representative of near-wall cell trajectories (*r* = 1.40 mm). The gradient is strongly localised in the axial direction, falling to less than 10% of its peak value within ±500 µm of the strut mid-plane. This spatial localisation has direct implications for trajectory dynamics: cells that enter the axial capture zone within a restricted window receive sufficient impulse to deviate toward the strut, while cells that pass at large axial offsets are effectively unaffected.

The profile is asymmetric about the strut mid-plane (*z* = 0), with the gradient rising steeply on the upstream side and decaying more gradually downstream, reflecting the geometric asymmetry of the rectangular prism near its ends. The full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the axial gradient profile is approximately 600 µm, which defines the effective axial interaction length for a cell in transit.

---

### 1.4 Magnetisation Sensitivity

**Figure 4** examines the dependence of the field and gradient magnitudes on the effective strut magnetisation *M*, swept over the range 0.1–2.0 MA/m. All field and gradient quantities scale linearly with *M* over this range, as expected from the linearity of the Akoun and Yonnet kernel with respect to magnetisation. Doubling *M* from 0.5 to 1.0 MA/m therefore doubles the gradient magnitude and quadruples the magnetic force (which scales as *M*²|**B**||**∇**|**B**||).

The COMSOL-calibrated effective magnetisation of *M*_eff = 2.20 MA/m (at *B*₀ = 1.5 T) falls at the upper end of the sweep range, confirming that full MRI-strength external fields bring the stent into a substantially enhanced operating regime compared to the zero-field case (*M*_eff = 0.619 MA/m). The linear scaling implies that magnetisation is the dominant single-parameter lever available to the clinician when optimising stent design within the constraints of biocompatible materials.

---

### 1.5 Geometric Parameter Sensitivity

**Figure 5** presents a sensitivity analysis of the gradient magnitude to the three strut dimensions: width *w*, thickness *t*, and length *L*. Each parameter is varied individually by ±50% about the nominal values (*w* = 100 µm, *t* = 80 µm, *L* = 500 µm) while all others are held fixed, and the peak gradient magnitude at *r* = 1.40 mm is recorded.

The results demonstrate that strut thickness *t* has the strongest influence on near-field gradient: a 50% increase in *t* raises the peak gradient by approximately 35%, while the same proportional change in *w* yields only a 12% increase. Length *L* has the weakest effect beyond a saturation length of approximately 400 µm, above which additional axial material contributes negligibly to the gradient at the strut mid-plane. These findings are consistent with the dipolar field approximation, in which the effective magnetic moment scales more sensitively with cross-sectional area than with length when the strut is already longer than its transverse dimensions. The sensitivity hierarchy (*t* > *w* > *L*) provides practical guidance for iterative stent design: optimising strut thickness is the most efficient route to increasing capture force.

---

### 1.6 Strut-Count Sensitivity

**Figure 6** illustrates the effect of the number of struts *n* on the azimuthally averaged gradient magnitude, spanning the range *n* = 4–16. The average gradient increases monotonically with *n* up to approximately *n* = 10, beyond which the incremental benefit diminishes as inter-strut spacing decreases and field superposition becomes less constructive. At *n* = 12 (the default geometry), the average gradient at *r* = 1.40 mm is within 8% of the *n* = 16 maximum, indicating near-optimal performance. Below *n* = 6, the gradient drops sharply between struts, creating large azimuthal dead zones where cells pass without experiencing significant magnetic force.

This figure also reveals a secondary effect: the peak gradient at any single strut position is *reduced* for high *n* due to the competing influence of adjacent struts' stray fields, which partially cancel the gradient component directed toward a given strut. The optimal *n* therefore depends on whether the design objective is maximising average capture probability (favouring higher *n*) or maximising force at individual strut sites (favouring intermediate *n* ≈ 8–12).

---

### 1.7 Two-Dimensional Gradient Contours

**Figure 7** presents a two-dimensional contour map of the gradient magnitude |**∇**|**B**|| in the radial–axial (*r*–*z*) plane evaluated at the azimuthal position of a strut centre. The contours delineate iso-gradient surfaces at 40, 100, and 300 T/m, which serve as reference thresholds throughout this work (see Section 5).

The 40 T/m contour — representing the minimum gradient required to exert a force comparable to Stokes drag on a 50 pg SPION-labelled cell — extends to a radial distance of approximately 380 µm from the strut surface at *B*₀ = 1.5 T. The 300 T/m contour, corresponding to forces sufficient for capture even at elevated flow velocities, is confined to within approximately 105 µm of the strut surface. The elongated axial extent of all contours (approximately 1.0–1.5 mm) relative to their radial extent reflects the strut's high aspect ratio and is directly responsible for the sustained axial interaction window identified in Figure 3.

---

### 1.8 Force-Parameter Spatial Map (Stage 1)

**Figure 8** maps the force parameter FP = |**B**||**∇**|**B**|| (units T²/m) across the *r*–*z* plane. This composite quantity is directly proportional to the magnetic force on a SPION-labelled cell (Section 2.2) and provides an integrated view of where force is concentrated. The map confirms that the force parameter peaks immediately adjacent to the strut surface and decays sharply in both the radial and axial directions, with a spatial structure closely following the gradient contours of Figure 7. The map also reveals a secondary local maximum approximately one strut width away in the azimuthal direction, arising from field superposition effects in the ring geometry.

---

### 1.9 Axial Gradient Scan

**Figure 9** complements Figure 3 by presenting the full axial gradient profile from *z* = −1.5 mm to *z* = +1.5 mm (relative to the strut mid-plane) evaluated at three representative radial positions (*r* = 1.35, 1.40, 1.45 mm). The profiles illustrate how the effective capture window narrows with increasing radial distance from the strut: a cell at *r* = 1.35 mm encounters gradients above 40 T/m over an axial window of approximately 900 µm, while at *r* = 1.45 mm the same threshold is exceeded over only 200 µm. This position dependence is a critical input to the trajectory analysis, where cells at different initial radial positions experience qualitatively different axial force profiles during their transit past the strut.

---

### 1.10 Finite-Difference Convergence Verification

**Figure 10** presents the convergence study for the central finite-difference gradient computation, plotting the relative error in |**∇**|**B**|| as a function of the step size δ over the range δ = 100 nm to 10 µm. The error follows the expected second-order convergence behaviour (*O*(δ²)) over three decades in step size, confirming the consistency of the numerical implementation. The optimal step size, balancing truncation error (decreasing with smaller δ) against floating-point cancellation error (increasing with smaller δ), is found at δ ≈ 500 nm, where the relative error falls below 0.01%. All subsequent gradient computations use this value.

This verification is non-trivial for the Akoun and Yonnet kernel, which involves near-singular logarithmic and arctangent terms at small distances from the prism surface. The clean convergence profile confirms that numerical cancellation is negligible at the evaluation points used throughout this work.

---

### 1.11 Radial–Axial Plane Heatmap

**Figure 11** shows a full heatmap of the magnetic flux density magnitude |**B**| (as opposed to |**∇**|**B**|| shown in Figure 7) in the *r*–*z* plane. The field is highest immediately adjacent to the strut surface and decays to background levels within approximately 500 µm radially. The heatmap clarifies that the field magnitude in the central vessel lumen (*r* < 1.0 mm) remains below 5 mT across all axial positions, confirming that magnetic force is negligible everywhere except in the near-wall region and that the approximation of undisturbed Poiseuille flow at the centreline is physically justified.

---

### 1.12 External Field Effect

**Figure 12** compares the gradient magnitude profile with and without an externally applied uniform axial field, at *B*₀ = 0 T (stent magnetisation only) and *B*₀ = 1.5 T (full MRI-strength external field). The external field amplifies the effective strut magnetisation via the soft-ferromagnet response of the stent material, increasing the gradient by a factor of approximately 3.55× at *B*₀ = 1.5 T relative to *B*₀ = 0 (calibrated against COMSOL; see Section 5). This amplification has a non-linear character: the gradient enhancement is not simply additive because the external field also raises the background |**B**| everywhere, modifying the direction of **∇**|**B**_total**| relative to **∇**|**B**_stent**| through projection effects.

This result directly motivates the use of MRI-guided deployment as the primary operational mode: the substantial gradient amplification available at clinical field strengths brings the effective capture radius from sub-100 µm (at *B*₀ = 0) to over 380 µm (at *B*₀ = 1.5 T).

## 2. Magnetic Force and Drag Analysis

Having characterised the magnetic field environment, this section quantifies the mechanical forces governing cell capture. The magnetic force on a SPION-labelled endothelial cell is computed using the formulation of Furlani and Ng (2006):

$$F_{\text{mag}} = \frac{V_{\text{SPION}} \, \chi_{\text{eff}}}{\mu_0} \, |\mathbf{B}| \, |\nabla|\mathbf{B}||$$

where *V*_SPION is the SPION volume corresponding to a given iron-oxide loading (pg per cell), χ_eff is the effective magnetic susceptibility (incorporating Langevin saturation, Section 6), and µ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m is the permeability of free space. The hydrodynamic drag is modelled as Stokes drag on a spherical cell of radius *R*_cell = 10 µm in a blood-viscosity fluid (η = 4 mPa·s):

$$F_{\text{drag}} = 6\pi \eta R_{\text{cell}} \, v_{\text{blood}}$$

where *v*_blood is the local blood velocity from the Poiseuille profile with mean velocity $\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s in a vessel of radius *R* = 1.54 mm.

---

### 2.1 Force-Parameter Map with SPION Loading

**Figure 13** reproduces the force-parameter map FP = |**B**||**∇**|**B**|| including explicit SPION loading dependence for both 10 pg/cell and 50 pg/cell loadings. Because the force parameter is independent of the SPION loading (it depends only on the field environment), the maps are identical in spatial structure; however, overlaying the drag-equivalent contour for each loading level illustrates how the effective capture zone — defined as the region where *F*_mag > *F*_drag — expands substantially with increasing loading. At 10 pg/cell, the capture zone is confined to within approximately 40 µm of the strut surface at the nominal flow velocity; at 50 pg/cell this expands to approximately 97 µm.

---

### 2.2 Magnetic Force vs Radial Distance

**Figure 14** presents the magnetic force *F*_mag as a function of radial distance from the strut surface for four SPION loadings spanning the experimentally reported range (10, 50, 100, and 200 pg/cell), evaluated at the strut mid-plane (*z* = 0). Forces are expressed in piconewtons (pN). At the nominal loading of 50 pg/cell, the magnetic force at 100 µm from the strut surface is approximately 1360 pN (Langevin model), decreasing to approximately 45 pN at 300 µm — a 30-fold reduction over 200 µm of radial distance that reflects the steep spatial decay of the field gradient.

For comparison, the Stokes drag force at the near-wall Poiseuille velocity (*v* ≈ 0.05 m/s at *r* = 1.40 mm, $\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s) is approximately 19 pN. The magnetic force exceeds drag by a factor of approximately 70 at 100 µm stand-off distance, declining to unity at approximately 97 µm for a 50 pg loading — the static capture distance. The loading dependence is linear, since *V*_SPION ∝ loading and χ_eff varies slowly with loading at moderate field strengths.

---

### 2.3 Stokes Drag vs Flow Velocity

**Figure 15** shows the Stokes drag force *F*_drag as a function of mean flow velocity $\bar{v}$ (0.02–0.5 m/s) at *r* = 1.40 mm, where the Poiseuille profile gives a local velocity of approximately *v*_local = 0.25$\bar{v}$ at this radial position. The drag force scales linearly with $\bar{v}$ and ranges from approximately 2 pN at the slowest simulated velocity (0.02 m/s) to approximately 47 pN at the fastest (0.5 m/s). This range brackets the magnetic force at 100–200 µm stand-off distance for loadings of 10–200 pg/cell, confirming that the capture problem is genuinely competitive across the physiologically relevant parameter space.

The figure also illustrates the Poiseuille profile itself at the reference velocity, highlighting the no-slip boundary condition at the vessel wall and the associated shear layer in which cells are assumed to advect prior to stent deployment.

---

### 2.4 Static Capture Criterion Map

**Figure 16** presents the binary spatial map of the static capture criterion: each point in the *r*–*z* plane is coloured according to whether the local magnetic force exceeds the local Stokes drag force. Results are shown for two representative SPION loadings (10 pg/cell and 50 pg/cell) at the nominal flow velocity $\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s.

For 10 pg/cell loading, the capture zone is a narrow crescent of approximately 18–28 µm depth immediately adjacent to the strut surface. For 50 pg/cell, the zone expands to approximately 60–97 µm. Critically, both maps show that the static criterion predicts *zero* capture for cells originating beyond the outermost captured position — a binary prediction that motivates the trajectory-based approach of Section 3, which demonstrates that significant capture can occur well beyond this static boundary via accumulated radial drift.

## 3. Trajectory Integration and Capture Efficiency

This section presents the core dynamic analysis, replacing the static force-balance criterion with a trajectory integration framework. Cell motion is governed by a first-order overdamped ODE (inertial effects are negligible at Re ≈ 1.1):

$$\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \mathbf{v}_{\text{blood}}(\mathbf{r}) + \frac{\mathbf{F}_{\text{mag}}(\mathbf{r})}{6\pi\eta R_{\text{cell}}}$$

This equation is integrated using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta solver (SciPy RK45) with relative tolerance 10⁻⁶ and absolute tolerance 10⁻⁹. Integration terminates when the cell either contacts the strut surface (captured), exits the domain at *z* ≥ 2 mm (escaped), or contacts the vessel wall. The effective capture range is defined as the outermost initial radial position from which a cell is captured, determined by a binary search to 11 µm resolution.

---

### 3.1 SPION Loading Sweep — Static Capture Distance

**Figure 17** plots the static capture distance as a function of SPION loading over the range 10–400 pg/cell at the nominal flow velocity. The static capture distance increases sub-linearly with loading, from approximately 6 µm at 10 pg/cell to approximately 140 µm at 400 pg/cell, saturating above approximately 200 pg due to the spatial extent of the near-field gradient region becoming the limiting factor rather than force magnitude. This figure provides the baseline against which trajectory results are benchmarked in Figure 21.

The loading range 10–50 pg/cell represents current experimental benchmarks (Polyak et al., 2008; Cicha et al., 2010), while 200–400 pg/cell represents hypothetical future high-loading preparations. Even within the experimental range, the static capture distance (6–97 µm) is substantially smaller than the trajectory capture range (Section 3.5), underscoring the importance of the dynamic analysis.

---

### 3.2 Single-Cell Trajectory Visualisation

**Figure 18** illustrates a representative single-cell trajectory in three dimensions for a 50 pg/cell cell injected at *r*₀ = 1.41 mm, the outermost position from which capture occurs at the nominal conditions ($\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s, *B*₀ = 0.5 T). The trajectory is colour-coded by instantaneous radial velocity magnitude.

The key feature of this figure is the two-stage character of the trajectory. During the first phase (upstream approach, *z* = −1.5 mm to *z* ≈ −200 µm), the cell drifts quasi-adiabatically inward at a rate proportional to the local force-parameter gradient, accumulating approximately 170 µm of inward radial displacement before entering the high-gradient near-field region. During the second phase (*z* > −200 µm), the rapid increase in gradient magnitude produces a strong inward acceleration culminating in strut contact. Without the first-phase accumulation, the cell would have passed beyond the capture zone — it is precisely this *ballistic* approach drift that the static criterion fails to account for.

---

### 3.3 Trajectory Bundle

**Figure 19** extends the single-trajectory analysis to a bundle of 20 cells injected at uniformly spaced radial positions from *r*₀ = 1.20 mm to *r*₀ = 1.45 mm. Captured trajectories are distinguished from escaping trajectories by colour. A heatmap projection in the *r*–*z* plane shows the spatial density of all trajectories, highlighting the convergence zone upstream of the strut.

The figure illustrates the sharp transition between captured and escaping trajectories: cells originating within approximately 50 µm of each other at *r*₀ = 1.40–1.45 mm diverge dramatically in outcome, with the innermost cells captured and the outermost escaping with minimal radial deflection. This sensitivity to initial radial position has implications for the efficiency estimates presented in Section 3.4: the capture probability is not a smooth function of injection position but transitions sharply near the critical radius.

---

### 3.4 Capture Efficiency Heatmap

**Figure 20** presents the two-dimensional capture efficiency *η*_cap as a joint function of mean flow velocity $\bar{v}$ (0.02–0.5 m/s) and SPION loading (10–300 pg/cell), computed over a sweep of 20 injected cells per parameter combination. The efficiency is expressed as a fraction of the total injected population.

Several features of this heatmap merit discussion. First, capture efficiency decreases with increasing flow velocity at fixed loading, as expected, but the decrease is less steep than a naive linear drag argument would suggest: doubling the velocity from 0.1 to 0.2 m/s reduces efficiency from approximately 70% to 50% (at 50 pg), a factor of only 1.4 rather than 2. This sub-linear dependence arises because higher velocities also increase the axial transit time over the capture zone, partially compensating the increased drag. Second, the efficiency surface shows a notable plateau at high loadings (> 150 pg) and low velocities (< 0.1 m/s), where essentially all near-wall cells are captured regardless of further loading increase, suggesting a saturation of the capture mechanism limited by the geometric extent of the near-wall injection layer rather than by force.

---

### 3.5 Static Criterion vs Trajectory Integration — Headline Comparison

**Figure 21** is the central result of this thesis, directly comparing the capture predictions of the static force-balance criterion (Furlani and Ng, 2006) and the trajectory integration framework. It comprises two panels.

**Panel (a)** shows the capture distance as a function of SPION loading (10–200 pg/cell) at the fixed nominal velocity $\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s. At 50 pg/cell loading, the static criterion predicts a capture distance of 6.8 µm, while trajectory integration yields 96.9 µm — an extension factor of **14.2×**. The discrepancy narrows at higher loadings as the static force exceeds drag over a larger region, but even at 200 pg/cell the trajectory capture range (approximately 190 µm) exceeds the static prediction by 3.7×.

**Panel (b)** shows the capture distance as a function of mean flow velocity (0.02–0.5 m/s) at a fixed loading of 50 pg/cell. Remarkably, the static criterion predicts *zero* capture at $\bar{v}$ ≥ 0.2 m/s (at this loading), because the drag force at the near-wall Poiseuille velocity exceeds the maximum available magnetic force at all accessible radial positions. In stark contrast, trajectory integration predicts a capture range of **96.9 µm** at $\bar{v}$ = 0.2 m/s, declining to approximately **74 µm** at $\bar{v}$ = 0.5 m/s — well above zero at all simulated velocities.

This finding constitutes the primary quantitative conclusion of the dynamic analysis: **trajectory integration is not merely a refinement of the static criterion but an entirely different physical prediction at physiologically realistic conditions**. The static criterion, applied in isolation, would lead to the erroneous conclusion that SPION-labelled endothelial cell capture is unachievable at normal cerebral blood flow velocities with 50 pg loading, when in practice the trajectory analysis predicts nearly 50% near-wall capture efficiency (Figure 20). The discrepancy arises from the physics of advective drift: a cell traversing the 2 mm strut interaction zone accumulates approximately 170 µm of inward radial displacement even when no single point along the trajectory satisfies the static force-balance condition.

## 4. VEGF Paracrine Transport

Having established the conditions under which endothelial cells are magnetically captured, this section investigates the therapeutic downstream consequence: the paracrine delivery of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) from the captured cell population to the surrounding vascular wall tissue. A two-dimensional reaction–diffusion–advection model is used:

$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D\nabla^2 C - \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla C + S(\mathbf{x}) - kC$$

where *C* is the VEGF concentration (ng/mL), *D* = 1.04 × 10⁻¹⁰ m²/s is the tissue effective diffusivity (Mac Gabhann and Popel, 2006), **u** is the tissue interstitial advection velocity, *S*(**x**) is the cell secretion source field (Gaussian kernel, *q*_cell = 0.068 molecules/s; Yen et al., 2011), and *k* = 1.93 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ is the first-order VEGF degradation rate constant (half-life ≈ 60 min). The diffusion length scale is *L*_D = √(*D*/*k*) ≈ 734 µm. The steady-state system is solved using a sparse direct linear solver; the transient system uses explicit Euler time-stepping with a CFL-limited timestep.

---

### 4.1 Steady-State Concentration Field

**Figure 22** presents the steady-state VEGF concentration field in the tissue slab for two secretion scenarios: basal secretion (*q*_cell = 0.068 molecules/s per cell, *N* = 320 captured cells) and gene-enhanced secretion (100× basal, *N* = 320 cells).

Under basal secretion, the maximum tissue concentration reaches only approximately 0.08 ng/mL — far below the minimum therapeutic band of 5–25 ng/mL established from the vasculogenic threshold criteria of Ozawa et al. (2004). The spatial distribution shows a diffuse, low-amplitude concentration landscape that decays to negligible levels within approximately 500 µm of the captured cell cluster, driven by the relatively strong VEGF degradation rate.

Under 100× gene-enhanced secretion, the maximum concentration rises to approximately 40 ng/mL, comfortably within the therapeutic band. The concentration contours now show a well-defined high-concentration zone of approximately 400–600 µm radius centred on the captured cell cluster. This result is consistent with the analytical diffusion-length estimate: at 100× enhancement, the effective source strength is sufficient to maintain concentrations above the 5 ng/mL threshold within one diffusion length *L*_D of the cluster.

This figure establishes a fundamental limitation of the basal-secretion scenario and motivates the use of gene-modified or pharmacologically stimulated cells with elevated VEGF secretion rates as the clinically viable approach.

---

### 4.2 Concentration vs Radial Distance

**Figure 23** quantifies the radial decay of the azimuthally averaged steady-state VEGF concentration from the captured cell cluster centre for both secretion scenarios. The basal profile decays from 0.08 ng/mL at the cluster centre to below 0.01 ng/mL within 300 µm, consistent with the exponential profile predicted by the one-dimensional reaction–diffusion solution *C*(*r*) ∝ *K*₀(*r*/*L*_D)/*L*_D² for a point source (where *K*₀ is the modified Bessel function of the second kind). The 100× enhanced profile decays from 40 ng/mL to 5 ng/mL at approximately 730 µm, closely matching the theoretical diffusion-length prediction.

The figure also marks the therapeutic window (5–25 ng/mL, shaded band), confirming that under enhanced secretion the therapeutic concentration is maintained over a tissue radius of approximately 500–730 µm — covering the relevant vascular-wall area in the context of stent-adjacent neovascularisation.

---

### 4.3 Transient Time-to-Threshold

**Figure 24** presents the transient VEGF concentration evolution at a representative tissue point 300 µm from the cluster centre, showing the time required to reach the therapeutic threshold of 5 ng/mL under the 100× enhanced secretion scenario. The concentration rises from zero to threshold in approximately 2.1 hours, reaching 90% of the steady-state value within approximately 4.5 hours.

The transient profile follows a characteristic reaction–diffusion kinetics curve: rapid initial rise driven by the high local source concentration, transitioning to a slower approach to steady state as the spatial concentration gradient equilibrates. The time-to-threshold of approximately 2.1 hours is clinically significant, as it indicates that the therapeutic effect begins within the same acute treatment window (4–6 hours) as the magnetic capture procedure itself — a favourable characteristic for intraoperative applications.

## 5. FEM Validation Against COMSOL

The analytical Akoun and Yonnet model treats each strut as a uniformly magnetised prism, which is an approximation to the physical behaviour of a soft-ferromagnetic stent strut that responds nonlinearly to the applied field through its *B*–*H* relationship. To assess the accuracy of this approximation under clinically relevant conditions, the analytical gradient predictions were benchmarked against finite-element method (FEM) simulations performed in COMSOL Multiphysics (Magnetic Fields, No Currents physics; relative permeability µᵣ = 2 for the stent material).

Calibration was performed by fitting the effective analytical magnetisation *M*_eff to match the COMSOL-predicted radial distance at which |**∇**|**B**|| crosses the 100 T/m threshold, independently at *B*₀ = 0 T and *B*₀ = 1.5 T.

---

### 5.1 Gradient Threshold Crossings — COMSOL Comparison

**Figure 25** presents the core FEM validation in three panels, comparing the analytical model against COMSOL at three gradient threshold levels (300, 100, and 40 T/m) for the twelve-strut ring geometry at *B*₀ = 1.5 T.

At the 40 T/m and 100 T/m thresholds — which correspond to the physiologically relevant capture zone for 50 pg and 200 pg loadings respectively — the analytical and COMSOL threshold crossing distances agree to within **0.4%** and **0.8%** respectively. Quantitatively:

| Threshold | Analytical crossing (mm) | COMSOL crossing (mm) | Error |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------|
| 40 T/m    | 0.3814                   | 0.3800               | +0.4% |
| 100 T/m   | 0.2382                   | 0.2400               | −0.8% |
| 300 T/m   | 0.1053                   | 0.1200               | −12.2% |

The 12.2% discrepancy at 300 T/m is confined to the immediate strut surface vicinity (*r* ≈ 105 µm from the strut), where the uniform-magnetisation approximation of the Akoun and Yonnet kernel begins to diverge from the true nonlinear field distribution in a saturated soft-ferromagnet. Since this region lies well within the strut wall thickness and is not traversed by cells in the trajectory simulations, this near-surface inaccuracy does not affect the capture predictions. All conclusions in Sections 3–4 are based on gradient values in the range 40–100 T/m, where the validated analytical model is accurate to within 1%.

The calibrated effective magnetisation values are *M*_eff = 0.619 MA/m at *B*₀ = 0 T and *M*_eff = 2.20 MA/m at *B*₀ = 1.5 T, corresponding to an external field amplification factor of 3.55× — consistent with the linear response expected from a soft ferromagnet at moderate relative permeability.

### 5.2 Multi-Geometry Sensitivity Comparison

**Figure 26** extends the COMSOL validation to four non-nominal geometries, comparing analytical and COMSOL predictions of the 100 T/m threshold crossing distance for perturbations in *n* (strut count), *w* (strut width), *t* (strut thickness), and *L* (strut length). These results confirm that the calibrated Akoun and Yonnet model is not overfitted to a single geometry but generalises accurately across the design space relevant to stent optimisation studies (Figure 5, Figure 6). The multi-geometry agreement provides additional confidence in using the analytical model for parametric sweeps, where FEM re-computation at each design point would be computationally prohibitive.

## 6. Langevin Saturation Model — Supplementary Analysis

The default magnetic force calculation in this work uses the Langevin saturation model to account for the nonlinear magnetisation response of SPIONs at elevated magnetic field strengths. The effective susceptibility is given by:

$$\chi_{\text{eff}}(B) = \chi_0 \cdot \frac{3\mathcal{L}(\xi)}{\xi}, \quad \xi = \frac{3\chi_0 B}{M_{\text{sat}} \mu_0}$$

where $\mathcal{L}(\xi) = \coth\xi - 1/\xi$ is the Langevin function, χ₀ = 2.0 is the low-field susceptibility, and *M*_sat = 446 kA/m is the bulk magnetite saturation magnetisation. At *B*₀ = 1.5 T, the field at the near-strut region approaches saturation, reducing χ_eff by up to 5.7× relative to its low-field value.

---

### 6.1 Saturation Impact on Force and Capture Predictions

**Figure Sat.** (supplementary) presents a three-panel comparison of the Langevin and constant-χ models: (a) the χ_eff(*B*) curve versus field magnitude, showing the saturation onset above approximately 0.3 T; (b) the magnetic force versus radial distance profiles for the two models at *B*₀ = 0.5 T; and (c) the static capture distance versus SPION loading sweep.

The quantitative impact of saturation on capture predictions is summarised in the table below:

| Metric | Langevin model | Constant-χ model | Reduction |
|--------|----------------|------------------|-----------|
| Static capture distance, 100 pg, *B*₀ = 0.5 T | 18.5 µm | 24.4 µm | 24% |
| Trajectory capture range, 50 pg, *B*₀ = 0.5 T | 96.9 µm | 114.3 µm | 15% |
| Capture efficiency, 50 pg, *v̄* = 0.2 m/s | 0.35 | 0.40 | 13% |
| Force at 100 µm stand-off, *B*₀ = 0.5 T | 1358 pN | 2337 pN | 42% |

The key insight from panel (c) is that the 5.7× reduction in χ_eff translates to only a **13–24% reduction in capture metrics**, depending on the metric considered. This relative insensitivity arises from the geometry of the problem: in the high-gradient near-field zone where capture is determined, the field magnitude is already high enough to saturate the Langevin function in both the Langevin and constant-χ formulations — the capture-determining region is not the highest-field zone (where the force difference is largest in absolute terms) but the outermost zone where *F*_mag ≈ *F*_drag (where both models are in moderate-field regime and closer in absolute value). The results confirm that the constant-χ model, while physically less rigorous, provides a conservative upper bound on capture predictions and is appropriate for order-of-magnitude feasibility estimates.

## 7. Advection Sensitivity — Supplementary Analysis

### 7.1 Tissue Interstitial Advection Impact on VEGF Transport

**Figure 27** examines the sensitivity of the steady-state VEGF concentration field to the tissue interstitial advection velocity **u**, which was neglected in the primary paracrine analysis of Section 4. Advection velocities from *u* = 1 µm/s (physiological lower bound) to *u* = 100 µm/s (pathologically elevated, post-stroke oedema) are considered.

The Péclet number for VEGF transport is Pe = *u* × *L*_D / *D* = *u* × 734 µm / (1.04 × 10⁻¹⁰ m²/s). At the physiological lower bound, Pe ≈ 0.007 (Pe ≪ 1), confirming that diffusion dominates and the zero-advection assumption of the primary analysis introduces negligible error. At *u* = 10 µm/s, Pe ≈ 0.07: a mild downstream asymmetry appears in the concentration field, displacing the concentration maximum approximately 50 µm in the downstream direction, but the maximum concentration and therapeutic radius are both altered by less than 5%. Only at *u* = 100 µm/s (Pe ≈ 0.7) does advection introduce a qualitatively different distribution, elongating the concentration plume in the flow direction and reducing the upstream therapeutic radius by approximately 30%.

These results confirm that the diffusion-dominated approximation adopted in Section 4 is valid under physiological conditions and remains accurate up to interstitial advection velocities of approximately 10 µm/s.

**Central conclusion.** The trajectory integration analysis demonstrates that the Furlani and Ng (2006) static force-balance criterion severely underestimates the effective capture range of SPION-labelled endothelial cells by a magnetisable stent at physiologically realistic cerebral blood flow conditions. At the clinically achievable SPION loading of 50 pg/cell and distal MCA flow velocity of 0.2 m/s, the trajectory model predicts an effective capture range of 96.9 µm and near-wall capture efficiency of approximately 45–50%, compared to the static criterion's prediction of 6.8 µm capture range and negligible efficiency — a discrepancy of 14.2×. This result arises from the physics of advective radial drift: cells accumulate significant inward radial displacement during their axial transit through the gradient field, even at positions where the instantaneous magnetic force does not exceed the drag force. The validated analytical field model (< 1% error at physiologically relevant gradients) and the Langevin saturation correction (13–24% reduction in capture metrics relative to the constant-susceptibility approximation) both strengthen the quantitative reliability of these predictions.
